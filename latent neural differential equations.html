What's this paper about?

Given any dataset of videos. We can make a realistic video that mimics the dataset. This method has been successfully applied to images before. One classic example is thispersondoesnotexist.com, where a face that's seemingly real is actually generated by a computer. My paper furthers previous literature by making time continuous rather than singularly sampled. To do this we apply some tricks from calculus to formulate the problem in multiple ways.

Redefining videos

Most people think of videos as flowing streams. In reality, they are simply a bunch of images stitched together.

Figure 1: Videos as a series of images

This creates the question of how to generate them. For all video generation models what happens is a series of points are turned into a series of images. That series of images is our video.

Points in space are a neighborhood

To best illustrate this topic we will think about driving through a neighborhood. Our model drives along the street of a neighborhood, and each house represents an image. In technical terms our model traverses a latent space mapping a point to an image.

Figure 2: A car driving through a neighborhood

Why old models stink

Old models only jumped from house to house and never drove. One moment they'd be at house one then the next at house two with no inbetween. It sounds illogical and it was illogical. Time isn't made of big jumps, it's like a flowing river. Figure 3 shows the results of thinking of time like previous works.

Figure 3: Hitting a house because it didn't move properly through time

Let's make it continuous

Instead of jumping inbetween time steps we can slowly drive between houses. This also gives us the ability if we want to visit intermediate houses rather than running around like a rabid squirrel.
